General notation

I will use the following notation throughout the course:

\(\boldsymbol{A} \ \equiv\) Capital bold symbols are used for tensors/matrices

\(\boldsymbol{v}\ \equiv\) Lowercase bold symbols are used for vectors

\(a \ \equiv\) Non-bold symbols will be use for scalars

Index notation

The index notation is a way to write expressions in a compact form.

Let \(\boldsymbol{x}\) be a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), then the \(i\)-th component of \(\boldsymbol{x}\) is \(x_i\) for \(i \in \{1, 2, 3\} \) or:
\(\boldsymbol{x}=\{x_1, x_2, x_3\}\)

Let \(\boldsymbol{S}\) be a tensor in \(\mathbb{R}^{3\times3}\), then the \(i,j\)-th component of \(\boldsymbol{S}\) is \(S_{ij}\) for \(i,j \in \{1, 2, 3\} \) or:
\(\boldsymbol{S}=\begin{bmatrix} S_{11}& S_{12}& S_{13}\\ S_{21}& S_{22}& S_{23}\\ S_{31}& S_{32}& S_{33}\end{bmatrix}\)
If an index is repeated in a term, then we assume summation over that index. For example,
\(x_i x_i = \sum_{i=1}^3 x_i x_i = x_1 x_1 + x_2 x_2 + x_3 x_3\)

Note that the result of the operation above is a scalar. we say that index \(i\) has contracted upon itself in the expression.

If the indices in a term are repeated more than twice, the expression is meaningless. Eg.
\(x_i x_i x_i\) is meaningless.

Example 1.1

Express a vector \(\boldsymbol{v}\) in terms of its components \(v_i\) and the unit basis vectors \(\boldsymbol{e}_i\) in index notation.

Solution: \(\boldsymbol{v} = v_1 \boldsymbol{e}_1 + v_2 \boldsymbol{e}_2 + v_3 \boldsymbol{e}_3 = v_i \boldsymbol{e}_i\)

Any vector \(\boldsymbol{v}\) can be expressed as a linear combination of the unit basis vectors \(\boldsymbol{e}_i\) as:
\(\boldsymbol{v}=v_i \boldsymbol{e}_i \)

Example 1.2

When an index appears in terms only once, we say that the index is free. Find the meaning of the expression \(\boldsymbol{c} \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j\).

Solution:
Because \(j\) is free, we can freely assign any value to it from {1, 2, 3} like.
\(\begin{align*} a_j=&\boldsymbol{c} \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j\\ a_1=&\boldsymbol{c} \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_1\\ a_2=&\boldsymbol{c} \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_2\\ a_3=&\boldsymbol{c} \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_3 \end{align*}\)

Example 1.2

Now let's use index notation properly
\(\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{c} \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j=& c_i \boldsymbol{e}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j\\ \end{align*}\)

Note that \(\boldsymbol{e}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j\) by itself has two free indices. As a result it is a matrix that returns 1 only if \(i=j\).
\(\boldsymbol{e}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j= \delta_{ij}= \boldsymbol{I}\)

where \(\delta_{ij}\) is the Kronecker delta and \(\boldsymbol{I}\) is the identity matrix.

Example 1.2

\(\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{c} \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j=& c_i \boldsymbol{e}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j\\ =& c_i \delta_{ij}\\ =& c_j \end{align*}\)

From this example we conclude that the Kronecker delta swaps the indices of a vector.
\(c_i \delta_{ij}= c_j \)

Example 1.3

Find the dot product of two vectors \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{v}\) in index notation.

Solution:
\(\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v} \cdot \boldsymbol{u}= & v_i\boldsymbol{e}_i \cdot u_j\boldsymbol{e}_j \\ =& v_i u_j \boldsymbol{e}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j \\ =& v_i u_j \delta_{ij} \\ =& v_i u_i \end{align*}\)

From this example we conclude that the dot product of two vectors is the sum of the products of their components.
\(\boldsymbol{v} \cdot \boldsymbol{u}= v_i u_i \)

Tensors

A tensor is a linear operator that maps a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) to another vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

In the expression:
\(\boldsymbol{v}= \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{u}\)
\(\boldsymbol{A}\) is a second-order tensor that takes \(\boldsymbol{u} \in \mathbb{R}^3\) and transforms it into \(\boldsymbol{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3\).

In index notation:
\(\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v}& =\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{u} \\ v_i \boldsymbol{e}_i& = \boldsymbol{A} u_j \boldsymbol{e}_j \\ \boldsymbol{e}_k \cdot v_i \boldsymbol{e}_i& = \boldsymbol{e}_k \cdot \boldsymbol{A} u_j \boldsymbol{e}_j \\ v_i \boldsymbol{e}_k \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_i& = u_j (\boldsymbol{e}_k \cdot \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{e}_j) \\ v_i \delta_{ki}& = u_j (\boldsymbol{e}_k \cdot \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{e}_j) \\ v_k& = u_j (\boldsymbol{e}_k \cdot \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{e}_j) \end{align*}\)

We show that term in RHS of the equation represent the component \(i, j\)th of tensor \(\boldsymbol{A}\).
\(A_{kj}= \boldsymbol{e}_k \cdot \boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{e}_j \)
In index notation, the action of a tensor \(\boldsymbol{A}\) on \(\boldsymbol{u}\) is given by:
\(v_i= A_{ij} u_j \)
The tensor product of two vectors \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{v}\) is defined as
\((\boldsymbol{u} \otimes \boldsymbol{v}) \cdot \boldsymbol{a}= (\boldsymbol{a} \cdot \boldsymbol{u}) \boldsymbol{v}\)

Let's express the definition in index notation:
\(\begin{align*} (\boldsymbol{u} \otimes \boldsymbol{v}) \cdot a_i\boldsymbol{e}_i&= (a_i \boldsymbol{e}_i \cdot u_j \boldsymbol{e}_j) v_k \boldsymbol{e}_k\\ &= a_i u_j v_k (\boldsymbol{e}_i \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_j) \boldsymbol{e}_k\\ &= a_i u_j v_k (\boldsymbol{e}_j \otimes \boldsymbol{e}_k) \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_i\\ &= u_j v_k (\boldsymbol{e}_j \otimes \boldsymbol{e}_k) \cdot a_i \boldsymbol{e}_i\\ \boldsymbol{u} \otimes \boldsymbol{v}&= u_j v_k (\boldsymbol{e}_j \otimes \boldsymbol{e}_k) \end{align*} \)

\(\boldsymbol{u} \otimes \boldsymbol{v}\) is a second order tensor with \(i, j\) components:
\((\boldsymbol{u} \otimes \boldsymbol{v})_{ij}= u_iv_j\)

From the last definition we also find an expression for tensors in index notation:

Any tensor \(\boldsymbol{A}\) can be expressed as:
\(\boldsymbol{A}= A_{ij} \boldsymbol{e}_i \otimes \boldsymbol{e}_j\)

Example 1.4

Find \(\boldsymbol{a} \otimes \boldsymbol{b}\) if \(\boldsymbol{a}=\{1, 3, 4\}\) and \(\boldsymbol{b}=\{2, 3, 2\}\).

Transpose tensor

The transpose of a tensor \(\boldsymbol{A}\) is defined as \(\boldsymbol{A}^T\):
\((\boldsymbol{A}^T)_{ij}= A_{ji}\)

Some useful identities

\(\boldsymbol{a}\cdot (\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{b})= \boldsymbol{b} \cdot (\boldsymbol{A}^T \boldsymbol{a})\)

\((\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{B})^T= \boldsymbol{A}^T \boldsymbol{B}^T \)

Symmetric and skew tensor

A tensor is symmetric if and only if \(\boldsymbol{A}^T= \boldsymbol{A}\).
A tensor is skew if and only if \(\boldsymbol{A}^T= -\boldsymbol{A}\).

Let us take any tensor \(\boldsymbol{A}\) and express it as:
\(\boldsymbol{A}= \frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{A}+ \boldsymbol{A}^T)+ \frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{A}- \boldsymbol{A}^T)\)

Then:
Any tensor can be decomposed into a symmetric and a skew tensor:
\(\boldsymbol{A}= sym\{\boldsymbol{A}\}+ skw\{\boldsymbol{A}\}\)
\(sym\{A\}\) is defined as:
\(sym\{\boldsymbol{A}\}= \frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{A}+ \boldsymbol{A}^T)\)
\(skw\{A\}\) is defined as:
\(skw\{\boldsymbol{A}\}= \frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{A}- \boldsymbol{A}^T)\)

Example 1.5

For the tensor:
\(\boldsymbol{A}=\begin{bmatrix} 1& 2& 3\\ 3& 5& 6\\ 2& 2& 1 \end{bmatrix}\)
Find \(\boldsymbol{A}^T\), \(sym\{\boldsymbol{A}\}\) and \(skw\{\boldsymbol{A}\}\).

Spherical and deviatoric tensors

A tensor is spherical if and only if \(\boldsymbol{A}= \alpha \boldsymbol{I} \ \ \forall \alpha \in \mathbb{R}\) .
The trace of a tensor is defined as:
\(tr(\boldsymbol{A})= A_{ii}\)
Any tensor can be decomposed into a spherical (\(\boldsymbol{S}_p\)) and a deviatoric (\(\boldsymbol{S}_q\)) tensor:
\(\boldsymbol{S}_p= \frac{tr(\boldsymbol{S})}{3} \boldsymbol{I}= \frac{1}{3}A_{ii}\delta_{jk}\)
\(\boldsymbol{S}_q= \boldsymbol{S}- \boldsymbol{S}_p\)

Example 1.6

For the tensor:
\(\boldsymbol{S}=\begin{bmatrix} 1& 2& 3\\ 3& 5& 6\\ 2& 2& 1 \end{bmatrix}\)
Find \(\boldsymbol{S}_p\) and \(\boldsymbol{S}_q\).

Cross product

\(\epsilon_{ijk}\) is defined as:
\(\epsilon_{ijk}=(\boldsymbol{e}_i \times \boldsymbol{e}_j)\cdot \boldsymbol{e}_k\)

Rapid inspection of the alternating symbol definition helps to determine that:
\(\epsilon_{ijk}= \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } ijk \text{ is an even permutation of } 1,2,3\\ -1 & \text{if } ijk \text{ is an odd permutation of } 1,2,3\\ 0 & \text{if } ijk \text{ is not a permutation of } 1,2,3 \end{cases}\)

Note that from the definition we can obtain the following valuable expression:
\(\boldsymbol{e}_i \times \boldsymbol{e}_j= \epsilon_{ijk} \boldsymbol{e}_k\)

Example 1.7

Using index notation find an expression for the cross product of two vectors \(\boldsymbol{u}\) and \(\boldsymbol{v}\).

Solution:
\(\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{u} \times \boldsymbol{v}&= u_i \boldsymbol{e_i} \times v_j \boldsymbol{e}_j \\ &=u_iv_j \boldsymbol{e_i} \times \boldsymbol{e}_j\\ &=u_iv_j \epsilon_{ijk} \boldsymbol{e}_k \end{align*}\)

From this example we conclude that the \(k\)th component of the cross product of two vectors is:
\((\boldsymbol{u} \times \boldsymbol{v})_k= u_iv_j \epsilon_{ijk} \)

Determinant of a tensor

We start with the premise that the following ratio is invariant regardless of choice of basis:
\(\cfrac{\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{u}\cdot (\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{v}\times \boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{w})}{\boldsymbol{u}\cdot (\boldsymbol{v}\times \boldsymbol{w})}\)

Recall that the value \(\boldsymbol{u}\cdot (\boldsymbol{v}\times \boldsymbol{w})\) is the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the vectors \(\boldsymbol{u}\), \(\boldsymbol{v}\) and \(\boldsymbol{w}\).

The determinant of a tensor \(\det(\boldsymbol{S})\) is the ratio of the volume of the parallelepiped defined by vectors \(\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{u}\), \(\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{v}\), and \(\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{w}\); and the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the basis \(\boldsymbol{u}\), \(\boldsymbol{v}\) and \(\boldsymbol{w}\).
\(\det(\boldsymbol{S})= \cfrac{\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{u}\cdot (\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{v}\times \boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{w})}{\boldsymbol{u}\cdot (\boldsymbol{v}\times \boldsymbol{w})}\)

Some useful properties which are given without proof:
\(\det(\boldsymbol{S}^T)=\det(\boldsymbol{S})\)
\(\det(\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{T})=\det(\boldsymbol{T}\boldsymbol{S})=\det(\boldsymbol{S})\det(\boldsymbol{T})\)
\(\det(\alpha\boldsymbol{S})=\alpha^3\det(\boldsymbol{S})\)
\(\det(\boldsymbol{S}^{-1})=\det(\boldsymbol{S})^{-1}\)

Example 1.8

Find an expression for the determinant of the tensor \(\boldsymbol{S}\) in terms of its components \(S_{ij}\).

Solution:
\(\begin{align*} (\boldsymbol{e}_p\times \boldsymbol{e}_r) \cdot \boldsymbol{e}_r \det(\boldsymbol{S})&= (\boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{e}_p\times \boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{e}_q) \cdot \boldsymbol{S}\boldsymbol{e}_r\\ \epsilon_{pqr} \det(\boldsymbol{S})&= (\boldsymbol{a}\times \boldsymbol{b}) \cdot \boldsymbol{c}\\ &= (a_i b_j \epsilon_{ijk})c_k\\ &= \epsilon_{ijk} S_{ip} S_{jq} S_{kr} \\ \epsilon_{pqr}^2 \det(\boldsymbol{S})&= S_{ip}S_{jq}S_{kr} \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{pqr} \\ \det(\boldsymbol{S})&= \frac{1}{6}S_{ip}S_{jq}S_{kr} \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{pqr} \end{align*}\)

The determinant of a tensor \(\boldsymbol{S}\) can be expressed as:
\(\det(\boldsymbol{S})= \frac{1}{6}S_{ip}S_{jq}S_{kr} \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{pqr}\)

Since the determinant of the tensor is needed to invert the tensor, we might express the components of the inverse tensor without proof.

\(S^{-1}_{ij}=\frac{1}{2} \frac{1}{\det(\boldsymbol{S})} \epsilon_{ikl} \epsilon_{jmn} S_{mk} S_{nl}\)

Tensor inner product

The double inner product of two tensors \(\boldsymbol{A}\) and \(\boldsymbol{B}\) is defined as:
\(\boldsymbol{A}:\boldsymbol{B}= Aij Bij \)

We now can measure the norm of a tensor using the following expression:

\( |\boldsymbol{A}|= \sqrt{\boldsymbol{A}:\boldsymbol{A}}= \sqrt{Aij Aij} \)

For the tensors:
\(\boldsymbol{A}=\begin{bmatrix} 1& 2& 3\\ 3& 1& 0\\ 0& 0& 1 \end{bmatrix}\)
\(\boldsymbol{B}=\begin{bmatrix} 1& 1& 1\\ 0& 1& 0\\ 0& 0& 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

Gradient of a tensor

Derivative operators can be applied to tensors in time and space. The former does not require many attention for now. However, spatial derivatives on vectors and tensors could be difficult to visualize.
Because there are three spatial dimensions we can construct an operator defined as:
\(\nabla=\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \right) = \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial}{\partial x_2}, \frac{\partial}{\partial x_3} \right)= \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\boldsymbol{e}_i \)
The gradient of a vector is defined as:
\(\nabla \boldsymbol{v}= \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_j}= v_{i,j}\)

Note that the gradient of a vector field is a second order tensor

Example 1.10

For the vector field \(\boldsymbol{v}= \{x^2, y^2, z^2\}\) find \(\nabla \boldsymbol{v}\).

Divergence

The divergence operator is defined as \(\nabla \cdot\) and applied to a vector field it is:
\(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{v}= \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_i}= v_{i,i}\)

Note that the divergence of a vector field is a scalar field. The divergence of a tensor field is a vector field

Example 1.11

Find the divergence of the same vector field as in the previous example. Also find the divergence of the gradient tensor of the same field.
