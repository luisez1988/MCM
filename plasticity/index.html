Yield surface and flow rule

Consider the block in the figure. Based on equilibrium if the force applied to block exceeds the friction resistance between the block and the surface, the block will slide.

That condition can be written as:
\(\sqrt{f_x^2+ f_y^2}= W\mu \implies f_x^2+ f_y^2= W^2\mu^2\)
\(W=\) weight of the block

Yield Surface

The circle represents all the possible values of the forces that can be applied to the block without causing sliding. This is called the yield surface.
We can express this condition generating a set function of the form:
\(F= W^2\mu^2 - f_x^2 -f_y^2 \)
\(F\) is the so called yield function

Normality

Note that if the block slides and the force is suddenly removed, the block will remain in the same position. The particle has attained a irreversible deformation.

What is the velocity direction of the block when it slides?

Let's use the most fundamental principle in the universe:
Nature always seeks the minimum energy state

Let's us write the rate of work of the system:
\( \begin{align} \dot{W}= \boldsymbol{f} \cdot \boldsymbol{v} \end{align} \)
This is subjected to the condition that the block is sliding. Thus we must use Lagrangian multipliers:
\( \begin{align} G= \boldsymbol{f} \cdot \boldsymbol{v} - \lambda F \end{align} \)

Now let's take the derivative with respect to \(f_x\) and \(f_y\) and equate both to zero
\( \begin{align} \frac{\partial G}{\partial f_i} = v_i - 2\lambda f_i =0 \implies v_i=2\lambda f_i \\ \end{align} \)

This result implies that the velocity is parallel to the force. Therefore, the force is normal to the yield surface.

The concepts we used at the microscale, can now be extended to the continuum by first generalizing the concept of yield surface.
A yield function has a geometry defined by a set of state parameters \( \boldsymbol{X}_s= \{X_{s1}, X_{s2}, X_{s3}..., X_{sn} \} \), and the stress tensor \( \boldsymbol{\sigma} \), such that:
\( \begin{align} F(\boldsymbol{X}_s, \boldsymbol{\sigma})< 0 & \implies \text{elastic state} \\ F(\boldsymbol{X}_s, \boldsymbol{\sigma})= 0 & \implies \text{yield/plastic state} \\ F(\boldsymbol{X}_s, \boldsymbol{\sigma})> 0 & \implies \text{impossible stress state} \\ \end{align} \)
During plastic loading we prevent \(F>0\) by imposing the consistency condition:
\( dF=0 \)

Analogous to the previous example we must ask ourselves the question: What is the direction of the strain rate tensor?

In an analogous fashion we will optimize the plastic work density of a material point:
\( \begin{align} \frac{\partial G}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}}= \frac{\partial }{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma} } ( \boldsymbol{\sigma}: \dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}^p )- \lambda \frac{\partial F( \boldsymbol{X}_s, \boldsymbol{\sigma} )}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma} }=0 \end{align} \)
\( \begin{align} \frac{\partial }{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma} } ( \boldsymbol{\sigma}: \dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p} ) &= \lambda \frac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma} } \\ \frac{\partial (\sigma_{ij}\dot{\varepsilon}^p_{ij} )}{\partial \sigma_{ij}} &= \lambda \frac{\partial F}{\partial \sigma_{ij} } \\ \end{align} \)

Flow rule

The previous result demonstrate that the plastic strain rate tensor is normal to the yield surface. This is called the flow rule.
\( \dot{ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} }^p= \lambda \frac{\partial F^*}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma} } \)

\(\lambda\) is called the plastic multiplier.
The * will let us understand an important concept later. Pretend it is not there for now.

We now have defined the existence of a yield function, the consistency condition, and the relation of the yield function with plastic strain increments. But how do we relate stress and plastic strains?

Stress-strain relationship

Consider a loading-unloading cycle in the figure. It is clear that the total strain at point A is of a recoverable or elastic deformation and a irreversible or plastic deformation. This principle applies to any strain increment, therefore:

\( \begin{align} \ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} & = \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} ^{el} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} ^p\\ d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} & = d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} ^{el} + d\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} ^p \end{align} \)

\( \begin{align} d\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^{el} \end{align} \)

\( d\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot( d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} - d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^{p})\)

Elasto-plastic constitutive matrix

We need to integrate the stress-strain relationship, flow rule, and consistency condition to find the elasto-plastic constitutive matrix.

\begin{align*} dF &= \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\sigma} + \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s} \cdot d\boldsymbol{X}_s = 0 \\ \end{align*}

\begin{align*} d \boldsymbol{X}_s&= \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s }{\partial \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p \end{align*}

\begin{align*} dF &= \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot (d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}-d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p ) + \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s} \cdot \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s }{\partial \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p = 0 \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p - \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s} \cdot \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s }{\partial \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p = \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \end{align*}

Now use flow rule:
\begin{align*} \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot \lambda \cfrac{\partial F^*}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} - \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s} \cdot \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s }{\partial \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p} \cdot \lambda \cfrac{\partial F^*}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} = \cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \end{align*}

Note that the term \( \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s}{\partial \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p}\) represents a hardening/softening rule , as it tells us how the state parameters change with plastic strain increments. A functional form to this parameter is often adopted empirically or based on physical interpretations.
\(H\): Is a measure of state parameter change related to hardening and softening processes.
\begin{align*} H= -\cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s} \cdot \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{X}_s }{\partial \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^p} \cdot \cfrac{\partial F^*}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \end{align*}
Therefore, we can now find the value of the plastic multiplier:
\begin{align*} \lambda = \cfrac{\cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} }{\cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot \cfrac{\partial F^*}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} + H} \end{align*}
Note that:
  • \(H>0 \ \implies\) Hardening
  • \(H=0 \ \implies\) perfect plasticity
  • \(H<0 \ \implies\) softening
Finally, replacing the value of the plastic multiplier in the stress-strain relationship we find the elasto-plastic constitutive matrix:
\begin{align*} d\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} - \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot \cfrac{\partial F^*}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \cfrac{\cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot d \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} }{\cfrac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} \cdot \boldsymbol{D}^{el} \cdot \cfrac{\partial F^*}{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}} + H} \end{align*}
Don't worry about the complexity of this equation. We will use it in a numerical integration algorithm that will make it easy to use.

von Mises model

The simplest of all elasto-plastic models. It is based in a limiting condition for the deviatoric strain. It is used for metals, undrained soil behavior, rocks.
For undrained soil behavior the limiting capacity is the undrained shear strength \(q=2S_u\). Thus
\(F= q - 2 S_u\)

Parameters needed: 2 elastic and 1 state par.
