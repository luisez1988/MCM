critical state theory

Critical state theory is a useful framework to describe the behavior of soils. It is based on the concept that at large strains, soils tend to evolve towards a critical state that is independent of the initial conditions.
Idea #1: At equal mean stress, materials with different initial density have equal ultimate strength.
\( q_c= M_c p'_c \)

\(M_c=\) critical friction ratio
Idea #2: At equal mean stress, materials with different densities have equal ultimate void ratio also called critical void ratio.
\( e_{c}(p')= \Gamma - \lambda \ln (p') \)
The equations from ideas #1 and #2 are used to define the critical state line.

State parameter

\( \psi:\) Is the vertical distance between the current void ratio and the critical void ratio.
\( \psi= e- e_c \)

critical state axioms

Apart from establishing the critical state line, critical state theory also provides a set of axioms that are used to describe the behavior of soils.
At large deformation, the deviatoric stress is steady.
\( \frac{\partial q}{\partial \varepsilon_q}=0 \)
At large deformation, the mean stress is steady.
\( \frac{\partial p}{\partial \varepsilon_q}=0 \)
At large deformation, there is no volume change.
\( \frac{\partial \varepsilon_v}{\partial \varepsilon_q}=D=0 \)

Stress-dilation

Now, we will use the energy density equation to derive a theoretical relationship between stress and dilation.

\( \begin{align} \dot{W}^p&= q \dot{\varepsilon}^p_q + p' \dot{\varepsilon}^p_v \\ \cfrac{\dot{W}^p}{p' \dot{\varepsilon}_q^p} &= \cfrac{q}{p'} + \cfrac{\dot{\varepsilon}_v^p}{\dot{\varepsilon}_q^p} \\ \cfrac{\dot{W}^p}{p' \dot{\varepsilon}_q^p} &= \eta + D^p\\ \end{align} \)

Where \( \eta \) is the stress ratio. Now, take the limit when \( \dot{\varepsilon}_q^p \rightarrow \infty \).

\( \begin{align} \lim_{\dot{\varepsilon}_q^p \rightarrow \infty} \cfrac{\dot{W}^p}{p' \dot{\varepsilon}_q^p} &= \lim_{\dot{\varepsilon}_q^p \rightarrow \infty} ( \eta + D^p ) \\ \lim_{\dot{\varepsilon}_q^p \rightarrow \infty} \cfrac{\dot{W}^p}{p' \dot{\varepsilon}_q^p} &= M_c \end{align} \)

At first Roscoe and Schofield proposed that \( M_c \) was a constant. This lead to the development of the Camclay model. Using this proposition we obtain:
\( M_c= \eta + D^p \)

Original Camclay model

The original Camclay model was proposed by Roscoe and Schofield in 1958. It is a critical state model with associative capped yield surface.

\( \begin{align} q &= \eta p' \\ \dot{q} &= \dot{\eta} p' + \eta \dot{p}' \\ \end{align} \)

By normality:
\( \begin{align} \frac{\dot{q}}{\dot{p}'} &= - D^p\\ \end{align} \)

\( \begin{align} -D^p \dot{p}' &= \dot{\eta} p'+ \eta \dot{p}' \\ (-M_c+ \eta -\eta) \dot{p}' &= \dot{\eta} p' \\ -\int \cfrac{\dot{p}'}{p'} &= \int \cfrac{ \dot{\eta} }{M_c} \\ \end{align} \)

\(-\ln(p')+C= \eta/M_c\)

Now we evaluate the previous equation with condition: \( p'=p'_c \) and \( \eta=M_c\). Where \(p'_c\) is the critical mean effective stress.

\begin{align} -\ln(p'_c)+C&= 1 \\ C&= 1 + \ln(p'_c) \\ -\ln(p')+1+\ln(p'_c)&= \eta/M_c \\ \end{align}

This is the OCC yield surface:
\( q= M_cp' \left[1 - \ln \left(\cfrac{p'}{p'_c} \right) \right] \)

NorSand model

The NorSand model developed by Jefferies in 1993 is a critical state model with associative flow rule.

The model uses the stress-path dependent model discussed in the previous section to generalize the model.

\( M(\theta)= M_{tc} - \cfrac{M_{tc}^2}{3+M_{tc}} \cos \left(\cfrac{3 \theta}{4} + \cfrac{\pi}{4}\right) \)

Where \(M_{tc}\) is the critical friction ratio for triaxial compression.

Minimum dilation

Further inspection of stress-dilation curves shows that dilation can not let pass of a minimum value.
From observations, the following relationship is proposed to regulate the minimum dilation in the model.
\( D_{min}= \chi_{tc} \psi_{Dmin} = \chi_i \psi_i \)

Where \( \chi_{tc} \) is the dilation coefficient for triaxial compression, \(\psi_{Dmin}\) is the state parameter at minimum dilation, \(\chi_i\) is the state dilatancy, and \(\psi_i\) is the image state parameter. We'll discuss the image condition in the next section.

Now let us define a better stress-dilation function and define the yield surface for NorSand.

Jefferies recognized that the normalized dissipation of work was not constant. He instead let it change and called it \(M_i\) to distinguish from the critical friction ratio:
\( M_i= \eta +D^p \)

Where \( M_i \) is the image friction ratio.

Furthermore, the slope at max stress ratio and min dilation is not 1:1. Thus they used Nova's volumetric coupling (\(N\)) to fix that problem using:
\(D^p_{min}= \cfrac{M_{c}(\theta) - \eta_{max}}{(1-N)}\)
Or:
\(M_i= -D_{min}^p (1-N) + M(\theta) + D^p_{min}\)

Recognizing that the minimum dilation needs 3D generalization, the following is proposed:

\( D^p_{min}= \cfrac{M(\theta)}{M_{tc}} \chi_i \psi_i \)

And then replacing in the previous equation:
\( \begin{align} M_i&= M(\theta) + D^p_{min}N \\ M_i&= M(\theta) \left(1+ \cfrac{\chi_i \psi_i N }{M_{tc}} \right) \end{align} \)

It is observed that a symmetric version of this equation yields better fit with data:
\( M_i= M(\theta) \left(1- \cfrac{N \chi_i |\psi_i| }{M_{tc}} \right) \)

NorSand yield function

Note that integration leads to a similar yield surface as for the OCC model

\( q= M_ip' \left[1- \ln \left(\cfrac{p'}{p'_i} \right) \right] \)

Where \(p_i\) is the pressure corresponding to the image condition of \(M_i\) or image mean stress.

Note that \(\psi_i=e-e_{ci}\), with \(e_{ci}= \Gamma -\lambda \ln(p_i)\) or:

\( \psi_i= \psi + \lambda \ln \left(\cfrac{p'_i}{p'} \right) \)

We still need to define an expression for \(\chi_i\) and define the maximum dilation condition in the yield surface.

Let's start writing the yield surface at minimum dilation:
\( \begin{align} \eta_{max}&= M_i \left[1- \ln \left(\cfrac{p'}{p'_i} \right)_{min} \right] \\ \eta_{max} -M_i&=- M_i \ln \left(\cfrac{p'}{p'_i} \right)_{min} \\ -D^p_{min} &= - M_i \ln \left(\cfrac{p'}{p'_i} \right)_{min} \end{align} \)

\( \begin{align} \cfrac{M(\theta)}{M_{tc}} \chi_i \psi_i &= M_i \ln \left(\cfrac{p'}{p'_i} \right)_{min} \\ \cfrac{p'_{min}}{p_i}&= \exp \left( \cfrac{M_{tc}}{M(\theta)M_i} \chi_i \psi_i \right) \end{align} \)

This leads to the minimum dilation extra yield surface:
\( p'_{min}= p_i \exp \left( \cfrac{\chi_i \psi_i}{M_{itc}} \right) \)
We can now find an expression for \(\chi_i\).

Start using the CSL equation to define: \( \begin{align} \psi_i&= \psi_{Dmin} + \lambda \ln \left(\cfrac{p'_i}{p'} \right)_{min} \\ \psi_i &= \psi_{Dmin} - \lambda \frac{D^p_{min}}{M_i} \\ 1&= \cfrac{\psi_{Dmin}}{\psi_i} - \lambda \frac{D^p_{min}}{M_i \psi_i} \\ 1&= \cfrac{\chi_i}{\chi_{tc}} - \lambda \frac{M(\theta) \chi_i \psi_i}{M_{tc}M_i \psi_i} \\ 1&= \chi_i \cfrac{M_{itc}- \lambda \chi_{tc}}{M_{itc}\chi_{tc}} \\ \end{align} \)

Finally:
\( \begin{align} \chi_i&= \cfrac{M_{itc}\chi_{tc}}{M_{itc}- \lambda \chi_{tc}} \\ \end{align} \)

Rearranging and particularizing for Tx compression:
\( \chi_i= \cfrac{\chi_{tc}}{1- \frac{\lambda \chi_{tc}}{M_{tc}}} \)

Hardening/softening rule

The original harding/softening rule of NorSand is the following
\(\cfrac{\partial p_i}{\partial \varepsilon^q_p}= h (p'_{i \ min} - p'_i )\)
Where \(h\) is the hardening parameter, which can be related to the initial state parameter as:
\( h= h_0 + b \psi_0 \)

Later empirical developments lead to the current version of the hardening/softening law:

\( \cfrac{\partial p_i}{\partial \varepsilon^q_p}= h \left( \cfrac{p'}{p'_i} \right)^2 \left[\exp \left(\cfrac{-\chi_i \psi_i}{M_{itc}}\right) - \cfrac{p'_i}{p'} \right] p'_i\)

Summary of NorSand Equations

Aspect of NorSand Equations
Internal model parameters \(\psi_i= \psi - \lambda \ln (p'_i/p')\) where \(\psi=e-e_c\)
\(\chi_i= \chi_{tc}/(1-\chi_{tc}\lambda/M_{tc})\)
\(M_i= M(1- \chi_i N |\psi_i| /M_{tc})\)
Critical state \(e_c= \Gamma - \lambda \ln(p')\)
\(M= M_{tc}- (M_{tc}^2/(3+ M_{tc})) \cos(3\theta/2 + \pi/4) \)
Yield surface and internal cap \(q= p'M_i[1- \ln (p'/p'_i)] \) with \(p'_{min}= p'_i \exp (\chi_i \psi_i/ M_{itc}) \)
Hardening rule \(\cfrac{\partial p_i}{\partial \varepsilon^q_p}= h \left( \cfrac{p'}{p'_i} \right)^2 \left[\exp \left(\cfrac{-\chi_i \psi_i}{M_{itc}}\right) - \cfrac{p'_i}{p'} \right] p'_i\)
Stress-dilatancy \(D^p= M_i -\eta \)
Elasticity Usually depending on confinement and void ratio

Strengths

  • Critical state based model
  • Unique set of parameters can simulate a large range of soil behavior.
  • Capable of accurately capturing soil dilation
  • Can be used for sands and clay
  • Can reproduce eodometric loading
  • Excellent for static liquefaction
  • Stress-path dependent

Limitations

  • As it is can not capture liquefaction triggering
  • Expensive to calibrate
  • Moderately difficult to implement
  • Non-linearity of model makes it slow compared to previous models.
  • Can not capture more advanced features such as particle breakage, cementation, viscosity, suction.
