What is the continua?

It is an idealization of matter substances that assumes it is continuos at all scales.

In a continuum with volume defined by solid region \(\Omega\) with closed surface \(\partial \Omega\), it is always possible to find a subregion \(\mathcal{B} \subset \Omega \) with closed surface \(\partial \mathcal{B}\) that is also a continuum body.

In reality if we keep "zooming in" we will eventually reach a point where the matter is made of particles. Eg.
  • Soils (particle level)
  • Molecules (molecular level)
  • Atoms (atomic level)
  • Fundamental particles (quantum level)
We hypothesize that matter is a continuum if the smallest of the large-scale characteristic lengths of a body (\(L_{min}\)) are much larger than the largest of the small-scale characteristic lengths of the microscopic level (\(D_{max}\)).
\(L_{min}>> D_{max}\)

Body configurations

A body in its reference configuration undergoes a deformation mapped by the deformation function \(\boldsymbol{x}=\chi(\boldsymbol{X}, t)\), that takes material point coordinates \(\boldsymbol{X}\) and transforms them into spatial point coordinates \(\boldsymbol{x}\).

Body configurations

The displacement field \(\boldsymbol{u}\) defines the displacement of a material point from its reference position to its current position.
\(\boldsymbol{u}=\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{X}\)

Time differentiation

Are properties that are independent of the amount of matter in the body and are trackable for each material point. Eg. displacement, velocity, acceleration, temperature, pressure, etc.
We can differentiate scalar, vector, and tensor fields with respect to time following a material point a.k.a. Lagrangian description of movement or a spatial point a.k.a. Eulerian description of movement as follows:

We can denote the rate of change of any intensive property of a material point as:
\(\dot{\alpha}= \frac{D \alpha}{Dt}, \ \ \dot{\boldsymbol{a}}=\frac{D \boldsymbol{a}}{Dt}, \ \ \dot{\boldsymbol{S}}=\frac{D \boldsymbol{S}}{Dt}\)
To achieve this we fixe position \(\boldsymbol{X}\).

Note that we can now define the material point velocity and acceleration as:
\(\boldsymbol{v}( t)= \dot{\boldsymbol{u}}= \frac{D\boldsymbol{u}}{Dt} \)

\(\boldsymbol{a( t)}= \dot{\boldsymbol{v}}= \frac{D\boldsymbol{v}}{Dt} \)

This are Lagrangian variables. But why if we want to track then stationed in a fixed point?

Realize that when tracking a material point we let for example \(\boldsymbol{v}(t)\) be a function of time only. We now are interested in tracking the property at the Eulerian point \(\boldsymbol{x}\) or \(\boldsymbol{v}(\boldsymbol{x}, t)\):

\( \begin{align*} \frac{D \boldsymbol{u}^m(t)} {Dt}&= \cfrac{\partial}{\partial t} (\boldsymbol{u^s}(\chi(\boldsymbol{X}, t),t)) \\ &= \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{u^s}}{\partial t} + \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{u^s}}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}} \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{X}}{\partial t} \\ \end{align*}\)

\(\frac{D}{Dt}\): is an operator that allows changing from a Lagrangian description to an Eulerian description.
\(\cfrac{D (*)}{Dt}= \cfrac{\partial (*)}{\partial t} + \boldsymbol{v}^s \cdot \nabla (*)\)

Rigid rotation

\(\boldsymbol{x}= \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{X} \implies \boldsymbol{u}= (\boldsymbol{Q}-\boldsymbol{I})\boldsymbol{X}\)
\(\boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{Q}^T=\boldsymbol{I}\)

Translation and rotation

\(\boldsymbol{x}= \boldsymbol{Q}\boldsymbol{X}+\boldsymbol{c}\)
\(\implies \boldsymbol{u}= (\boldsymbol{Q}-\boldsymbol{I})\boldsymbol{X}+\boldsymbol{c} \)

Deformation and strain

Recall:
\(\boldsymbol{x}= \chi(\boldsymbol{X}, t)\)
\(d \boldsymbol{x}= \cfrac{\partial \chi(\boldsymbol{X}, t)}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}} d \boldsymbol {X} \)
\(d \boldsymbol{x}= \boldsymbol{F} d \boldsymbol {X} \)

We can define the deformation gradient tensor as:
\(\boldsymbol{F}= \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{x}}{\partial \boldsymbol{X}} \)

\(\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{X}+\boldsymbol{u}(\boldsymbol{X})+ d \boldsymbol{x} &= \boldsymbol{X}+d \boldsymbol{X}+ \boldsymbol{u}(\boldsymbol{X}+d \boldsymbol{X}) \\ \boldsymbol{u}(\boldsymbol{X})+ d \boldsymbol{x} &= \boldsymbol{u}(\boldsymbol{X}+d \boldsymbol{X})+d \boldsymbol{X} \\ d \boldsymbol{x}&= d \boldsymbol{X} + \boldsymbol{u}(\boldsymbol{X}+d \boldsymbol{X})- \boldsymbol{u}(\boldsymbol{X})\\ d \boldsymbol{x}&= d \boldsymbol{X} + \nabla \boldsymbol{u} d \boldsymbol{X}\\ d \boldsymbol{x}&= (\nabla \boldsymbol{u}+ \boldsymbol{I}) d \boldsymbol{X} \end{align*} \)

This implies that:
\(\boldsymbol{F}= \nabla \boldsymbol{u}+ \boldsymbol{I} \)
Note that we still require a way to measure the "amount" of deformation. We can do this taking the ratio of the magnitudes of the deformation over the original length of a fiber.
\(\varepsilon=\frac{\Delta L}{L_0}\)

\(\begin{align*} d \boldsymbol{x} \cdot d \boldsymbol{x}&= \boldsymbol{F} d \boldsymbol{X} \cdot \boldsymbol{F} d \boldsymbol{X} \\ || d \boldsymbol{x} ||^2&= d \boldsymbol{X} \cdot \boldsymbol{F}^T \boldsymbol{F} \cdot d \boldsymbol{X} \\ \end{align*} \)

\(\boldsymbol{C}= \boldsymbol{F}^T\boldsymbol{F}\)
Note that if \(\boldsymbol{F}^T\boldsymbol{F}=\boldsymbol{I} \implies || d \boldsymbol{x} ||^2= || d \boldsymbol{X} ||^2\) or rigid motion

\(\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{C}&= \boldsymbol{F}^T\boldsymbol{F} \\ &= (\nabla \boldsymbol{u}+ \boldsymbol{I})^T(\nabla \boldsymbol{u}+ \boldsymbol{I}) \\ &= \nabla \boldsymbol{u}^T \nabla \boldsymbol{u}+ \nabla \boldsymbol{u}^T + \nabla \boldsymbol{u} + \boldsymbol{I} \end{align*} \)

\(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}= \frac{1}{2}(\nabla \boldsymbol{u}+ \nabla \boldsymbol{u}^T + \nabla \boldsymbol{u}^T \nabla \boldsymbol{u})\)
This tensor is:
  • Symmetric
  • Valid for finite deformations

Small (Infinitesimal) strain tensor

As mentioned before \(\boldsymbol{u}\) is defined between two "snapshots" indifferent of time. In some engineering applications, and more importantly in numerical modelling we calculate change in very small or infinitesimal increments. Therefore, we can safely assume that \(\nabla \boldsymbol{u}^T \nabla \boldsymbol{u} \approx \boldsymbol{0}\)
\(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}= \frac{1}{2}(\nabla \boldsymbol{u}+ \nabla \boldsymbol{u}^T )\)

Note that \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\) is the symmetric part os the displacement gradient.

\( \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial X_1} & \frac{1}{2}(\frac{\partial u_1}{\partial X_2}+\frac{\partial u_2}{\partial X_1}) & \frac{1}{2}(\frac{\partial u_1}{\partial X_3}+\frac{\partial u_3}{\partial X_1}) \\ \frac{1}{2}(\frac{\partial u_1}{\partial X_2}+\frac{\partial u_2}{\partial X_1}) & \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial X_2} & \frac{1}{2}(\frac{\partial u_2}{\partial X_3}+\frac{\partial u_3}{\partial X_2}) \\ \frac{1}{2}(\frac{\partial u_1}{\partial X_3}+\frac{\partial u_3}{\partial X_1}) & \frac{1}{2}(\frac{\partial u_2}{\partial X_3}+\frac{\partial u_3}{\partial X_2}) & \frac{\partial u_3}{\partial X_3} \end{bmatrix} \)

Note that the trace of the strain tensor is the sum of the axial strains, which is equal to the volumetric strain (\(\varepsilon_v\)) also is the divergence of the displacement vector field:
\(tr(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})= \varepsilon_{ii}= \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{u}= \varepsilon_v\)
We can now decompose the strain tensor into its volumetric and deviatoric parts:
\(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}= \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}- \varepsilon_v \boldsymbol{I}\)

Note that the deviatoric strain tensor represents the distortions of the body, while the volumetric strain tensor the dilation or compression of the body.

Deviatoric strain

The invariants of a tensor are scalar values that are independent of the coordinate system. In constitutive modeling, we use invariants because it facilitates and reduce the computational cost associated with matrix operations.

Note that the trace of a tensor is invariant. Besides the norm of the deviatoric tensor is also an invariant. Furthermore, any operation and combination of invariants is also an invariant.

\(\varepsilon_q= \sqrt{2/3 ||\boldsymbol{\epsilon}||}=\sqrt{2/3 \boldsymbol{\epsilon}:\boldsymbol{\epsilon}} \)

The velocity gradient tensor

The velocity gradient tensor is defined with the spatial coordinates as:
\(\boldsymbol{L}= \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{v}^s}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}} \)

The differential of the velocity could be express ass:
\(\begin{align*} d \boldsymbol{v}^s&= \cfrac{\partial \boldsymbol{v}^s}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}} d \boldsymbol{x} \\ &= \boldsymbol{L} d \boldsymbol{x} \end{align*}\)

Strain rate tensor

We can now decompose \(\boldsymbol{L}\) into a symmetric and skew part.

Strain rate tensor

Is the symmetric part of \(\boldsymbol{L}\):
\(\boldsymbol{D}= sym(L)= \frac{1}{2}(\nabla_x \boldsymbol{v}^s + \nabla_x \boldsymbol{v}^{sT})\)

Measures deformations around a fixed Eulerian point

Spin tensor

Is the skew part of \(\boldsymbol{L}\):
\(\boldsymbol{W}= skw(L)= \frac{1}{2}(\nabla_x \boldsymbol{v}^s - \nabla_x \boldsymbol{v}^{sT})\)

Measures vorticity around a fixed Eulerian point

But we want to find the Lagrangian strain rate.

Let's start measuring the rate of change of a fiber in the current configuration:
\( \begin{align} \frac{\partial (d \boldsymbol{x} \cdot d \boldsymbol{x})}{\partial t} &= d \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{x} }{\partial t} \cdot d \boldsymbol{x} + d \boldsymbol{x} \cdot d \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{x} }{\partial t} \\ &= d \boldsymbol{v}^s \cdot d \boldsymbol{x} + d \boldsymbol{x} \cdot d \boldsymbol{v}^s \\ &= ( d \boldsymbol{x} \boldsymbol{L} ) \cdot d \boldsymbol{x} + d \boldsymbol{x} \cdot ( \boldsymbol{L} d \boldsymbol{x} ) \\ &= d \boldsymbol{x} \cdot (\boldsymbol{L}^T+ \boldsymbol{L}) \cdot d \boldsymbol{x} \\ &= 2 d \boldsymbol{x} \cdot \boldsymbol{D} \cdot d \boldsymbol{x} \\ \end{align} \)

I will give without proof the following relationship:
\(\frac{\partial d\boldsymbol{x} \cdot d\boldsymbol{x} }{\partial t}= 2 d \boldsymbol{X} \cdot \dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}} \cdot d \boldsymbol{X}\)

\( \begin{align} d \boldsymbol{X} \cdot \dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}} \cdot d \boldsymbol{X} &= d \boldsymbol{x} \cdot \boldsymbol{D} \cdot d \boldsymbol{x}\\ &= d \boldsymbol{X} \cdot \boldsymbol{F}^T \cdot \boldsymbol{D} \cdot \boldsymbol{F} \cdot d \boldsymbol{X} \\ \end{align} \)

We now have an expression relating the Lagrangian and Eulerian strain rate tensors:
\(\dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}= \boldsymbol{F}^T \cdot \boldsymbol{D} \cdot \boldsymbol{F} \)
Note that for infinitesimal deformation, the deformation gradient tensor approaches the identity matrix, and the Lagrangian and Eulerian strain rate tensors are equal.

You should be able to proof that:

\( \dot{\varepsilon}_v= \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{v}= tr(\dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}) \)

Forces in a body

\(\boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{X}, t)\): Body forces act internally and are produced by an external field like gravity.
\( \boldsymbol{f}_v= \int_{\Omega} \rho \boldsymbol{b}(\boldsymbol{X}, t) dV \)
\(\boldsymbol{t}(\boldsymbol{X}, t)\): Act externally on the surface of the body. Eg. reactions or contact forces.
\( \boldsymbol{f}_s= \int_{\partial \Omega} \boldsymbol{t}(\boldsymbol{X}, t) dS \)

Cauchy's theorem

For any point \(\boldsymbol{X} \in \Omega\) there exists a stress tensor that acts on the set of planes with normal \(\boldsymbol{n}\) that pass trough \(\boldsymbol{X}\) such that:
\( \boldsymbol{t}= \boldsymbol{\sigma} \boldsymbol{n} \)
\(\mathbf{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{11} & \tau_{12} & \tau_{13} \\ \tau_{21} & \sigma_{22} & \tau_{23} \\ \tau_{31} & \tau_{32} & \sigma_{33} \end{bmatrix} \)
What if we want to find the traction that vanishes all shear stresses in a certain plane?

\( \begin{align} \boldsymbol{t}&= \alpha \boldsymbol{n} \\ \boldsymbol{\sigma} \cdot \boldsymbol{n}&= \alpha \boldsymbol{n} \\ \boldsymbol{\sigma} \cdot \boldsymbol{n} -\alpha \boldsymbol{n}&= \boldsymbol{0} \\ (\boldsymbol{\sigma} -\alpha \boldsymbol{I}) \cdot \boldsymbol{n}&= \boldsymbol{0} \\ (\boldsymbol{\sigma} -\alpha \boldsymbol{I}) &= \boldsymbol{0} \end{align} \)

The principal stresses of a stress tensor are the eigenvalues of the stress tensor, and the principal directions are the eigenvectors of the stress tensor.

Stress invariants

Note that the principal stresses are invariants of the stress tensor. Recall that the trace of the tensor is also invariant.
\(p\): is defined as:
\( p=\frac{1}{3} tr(\boldsymbol{\sigma})= \frac{1}{3} \sigma_{ii}\)
\( \boldsymbol{\sigma}^q= \boldsymbol{\sigma} - p \boldsymbol{I} \)
\(q\): is defined as:
\( q= \sqrt{\frac{3}{2} \boldsymbol{\sigma}^q: \boldsymbol{\sigma}^q}= \sqrt{\frac{3}{2} \sigma^q_{ij}\sigma^q_{ij}} \)

Principle of effective stresses

\(\boldsymbol{\sigma}'\):
\( \boldsymbol{\sigma}'= \boldsymbol{\sigma}- u \boldsymbol{I} \)

\(u\)= Pore pressure

\( \begin{align} p'&=\frac{1}{3} [tr(\boldsymbol{\sigma}')= tr(\boldsymbol{\sigma})- tr(u \boldsymbol{I})] \\ &=\frac{1}{3} [tr(\boldsymbol{\sigma})- 3u] \\ &= p- u \end{align} \)

The mean effective stress is:
\( p'=p-u \)

\( \begin{align} \boldsymbol{\sigma}_q' &= \boldsymbol{\sigma}'- p' \boldsymbol{I}\\ &= \boldsymbol{\sigma}- (p'+ u) \boldsymbol{I} \\ &= \boldsymbol{\sigma}- p \boldsymbol{I}\\ &= \boldsymbol{\sigma}_q \end{align} \)

Pore pressure does not affect deviatoric stresses.
